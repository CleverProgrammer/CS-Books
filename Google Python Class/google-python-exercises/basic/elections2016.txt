class="timestamp intro"></p><div class="body-text"><p><span class="drop-cap">B</span>efore any votes are cast, presidential candidates compete for the support of influential members of their party, especially elected officials like U.S. representatives, senators and governors. During the period known as the &ldquo;invisible primary,&rdquo; these &ldquo;party elites&rdquo; seek to coalesce around the candidates they find most acceptable as their party&rsquo;s nominee. Over the past few decades, when these elites have reached a consensus on the best candidate, rank-and-file voters have usually followed.</p><div class="updates"><h6>Latest Endorsements</h6><table><tr><td class="date">8/21</td><td class="description"><strong>Jeb Bush</strong> endorsed by <a href="http://www.citizen-times.com/story/elections/2015/08/21/patrick-mchenry-jeb-bush-presidential/32136313/" target="_blank">Rep. We use a simple weighting system: <strong>10 points for governors, 5 points for U.S. senators and 1 point for U.S. representatives</strong> (there are roughly five times as many representatives as senators and 10 times as many representatives as governors).</p></div></div><div id="nominees" class="clearfix"><h3>Current candidates, past nominees</h3><p class="chart-intro">Cumulative endorsement points of presidential nominees before they clinched &mdash; and how the 2016 candidates compare.</p><div class="graphs clearfix"><div data-party="D" class="graph"></div><div data-party="R" class="graph"></div></div></div><div class="container"><div class="body-text"><p>In the book &ldquo;<a href="http://www.amazon.com/Party-Decides-Presidential-Nominations-American/dp/0226112373" target="_blank">The Party Decides</a>&rdquo; (2008), the most comprehensive study of the invisible primary, the political scientists Marty Cohen, David Karol, Hans Noel and John Zaller evaluated data on endorsements made in presidential nomination contests between 1980 and 2004 and found that &ldquo;early endorsements in the invisible primary are the most important cause of candidate success in the state primaries and caucuses.&rdquo;</p><p>These endorsements can serve several purposes. In some cases, they directly influence voters who trust the judgment of governors and members of Congress from their party. In other cases, endorsements serve as a signal to other party elites. &ldquo;It tells others who is acceptable and who is unacceptable,&rdquo; <a href="http://www.martycohen.net/5.html" target="_blank">Cohen</a>, an associate professor of political science at James Madison University, said in an e-mail to FiveThirtyEight. &ldquo;This is the coordination process that we believe goes on during the invisible primary and by way of public endorsements that was formerly and more formally undertaken at the convention.&rdquo;</p><p>While each election cycle is different, nomination races since 1980 have fallen into two broad categories. In some &mdash; like the Republican contest of <span class="sparkline gop" data-cycle="1996R"><strong>1996</strong> </span>  and both the <span class="sparkline dem" data-cycle="2000D"><strong>Democratic</strong> </span> and <span class="sparkline gop" data-cycle="2000R"><strong>Republican</strong> </span> races of 2000 &mdash; party elites rallied around a candidate well before the Iowa caucuses, and these candidates went on to win their nominations with relative ease. In others, like the Democratic contests of <span class="sparkline dem" data-cycle="1988D"><strong>1988</strong> </span> and <span class="sparkline dem" data-cycle="2004D"><strong>2004</strong> </span>, there was less of an endorsement consensus, with party officials taking more of a wait-and-see approach until voters weighed in. Party elites coalesced around a candidate only after the Iowa caucuses and the New Hampshire primary.</p></div></div><div class="historical clearfix"><h3>The shapes of past endorsement
